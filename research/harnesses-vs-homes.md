# Harnesses vs Homes 
## Two Architectures for Artificial Intelligence

## Introduction

In recent years, a remarkable convergence has occurred in the design of advanced AI systems. Independent teams at leading research labs—OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, and others—have arrived at a strikingly similar architectural pattern for solving complex problems with large language models.

The pattern is familiar: break a problem into pieces, assign specialized agents to solve each part, verify the results, and repeat the cycle until a satisfactory answer emerges.

This approach has proven powerful. It allows a single language model to behave like a coordinated team.

But it also reveals something deeper about the current state of artificial intelligence. Increasingly, the capabilities of AI systems are not coming from the models themselves, but from the *infrastructure built around them*.

These infrastructures resemble elaborate exoskeletons—external control systems designed to amplify the raw capabilities of the model.

In this paper, we call these systems **harnesses**.

Yet there is another possible approach: rather than building increasingly complex harnesses around AI models, we can design environments where intelligence emerges naturally from the structure of the workspace itself.

In this paper, we call these environments **homes**.

The distinction between harnesses and homes reveals two fundamentally different architectures for artificial intelligence.

---

## The Rise of AI Harnesses

The dominant approach to advanced AI systems today is orchestration.

Large language models are embedded within increasingly complex frameworks that coordinate their behavior. These frameworks typically implement a recurring cycle:

1. **Decomposition**  
   A planner agent divides a complex task into smaller subtasks.

2. **Execution**  
   Specialized agents work on individual components of the problem.

3. **Verification**  
   A reviewing agent checks the outputs for correctness.

4. **Iteration**  
   The system repeats the process until the solution stabilizes.

The result is a distributed workflow that resembles a small organization. One agent plans, others execute, and another verifies.

This architecture is now widely visible in modern AI systems:

- multi-agent orchestration frameworks  
- automated coding assistants  
- research agents  
- autonomous task pipelines

These systems function like an **Iron Man suit** for artificial intelligence: an external harness that dramatically amplifies the model’s capabilities.

Harness architectures offer several clear advantages:

- they extend the capability of existing models
- they enable specialization between agents
- they provide error checking and iteration
- they allow systems to tackle larger problems

In many cases, they succeed where single-prompt approaches fail.

But harnesses also introduce a new class of problems.

---

## The Cost of Harness Complexity

As harness architectures grow more sophisticated, they accumulate layers of complexity.

A typical orchestration system may include:

- planning agents  
- execution agents  
- verification agents  
- memory stores  
- tool interfaces  
- workflow controllers  
- logging systems  
- evaluation pipelines

Each layer adds capability—but also fragility.

Harness-based systems often exhibit several common challenges:

**Operational complexity**  
Debugging multi-agent pipelines can be extremely difficult.

**Cost amplification**  
A single user request may trigger dozens or hundreds of model calls.

**Hidden state**  
The reasoning path becomes distributed across multiple agents and prompts.

**Architectural brittleness**  
Small failures in one component can cascade through the system.

In short, harnesses unlock capability, but they do so by constructing increasingly elaborate machinery around the model.

This raises an important question.

What if the intelligence we seek does not need to be *forced* through orchestration?

What if it could instead *emerge* from the environment itself?

---

## Homes: Intelligence Through Environment Design

An alternative architecture begins with a different assumption.

Instead of asking:

> How do we control the model more effectively?

we ask:

> What kind of environment allows intelligence to emerge naturally?

In a **home-based architecture**, intelligence is not imposed through orchestration. It arises from the structure of the workspace in which agents operate.

The environment itself provides:

- persistent memory
- structured relationships
- spatial organization
- contextual inheritance
- inspectable state
- automation triggers

Agents do not require an elaborate harness because the workspace already contains the information and structure they need.

The environment acts as a form of *cognitive scaffolding*.

Rather than constructing external workflows, the system organizes knowledge, tasks, and agents within a coherent space where reasoning becomes simpler.

In such environments, agents behave less like tools and more like **residents**.

They inhabit a workspace rather than being tightly controlled by an orchestration pipeline.

---

## Harnesses vs Homes

The difference between these two architectures can be summarized as follows.

| Harness Architecture | Home Architecture |
|---|---|
| Intelligence through orchestration | Intelligence through environment |
| External control systems | Structured workspaces |
| Many transient prompts | Persistent context |
| Distributed hidden state | Inspectable environment |
| Complex pipelines | Natural interaction |

Harness systems treat AI models as engines that must be constantly managed.

Home systems treat AI agents as inhabitants of a structured environment.

The distinction may appear subtle, but it has significant implications for how AI systems scale.

Harness architectures tend to accumulate complexity as new capabilities are added.

Home architectures accumulate *structure*.

---

## Environmental Intelligence

The concept underlying home architectures is sometimes called **environmental intelligence**.

In such systems, intelligence is not concentrated in a single component. Instead, it emerges from the interaction between agents and their environment.

The workspace itself becomes part of the cognitive system.

This idea has deep precedents in several fields:

- cognitive science  
- distributed cognition  
- systems theory  
- human organizational design

Human intelligence often relies on structured environments: notebooks, diagrams, libraries, workflows, and shared spaces.

These environments reduce the burden on individual reasoning.

Similarly, intelligent digital workspaces can provide structure that reduces the need for complex orchestration.

---

## Simplicity, Control, and Inspectability

One of the most significant advantages of home-based architectures is **inspectability**.

Harness systems often behave like black boxes composed of multiple interacting prompts and agents.

Tracing a failure can require reconstructing a complex chain of events.

In contrast, environment-based systems keep state in visible structures:

- files
- folders
- relationships
- logs
- triggers

The reasoning environment becomes navigable.

This makes the system easier to debug, modify, and extend.

Home architectures also offer a potential advantage in **cost and efficiency**.

Because intelligence is embedded in the environment, agents often require fewer orchestration steps to reach useful outcomes.

The workspace itself provides context that would otherwise require additional prompting and coordination.

---

## Agents as Residents

The shift from harnesses to homes reflects a deeper conceptual transition.

In harness architectures, AI systems are tools.

In home architectures, AI systems are participants in a structured world.

Agents interact with:

- documents
- projects
- workflows
- other agents
- shared knowledge

They operate within a persistent environment rather than being spun up for isolated tasks.

This model resembles human organizations more closely than orchestration pipelines.

People rarely solve complex problems through perfectly scripted workflows.

Instead, they operate within structured environments that support collaboration and reasoning.

Digital workspaces can provide similar support for AI agents.

---

## Toward Intelligent Environments

As AI systems mature, it is possible that the most effective architectures will combine elements of both approaches.

Harnesses may remain useful for highly specialized tasks.

But the broader direction of intelligent systems may move toward environments where intelligence is embedded in the structure of the workspace itself.

In such systems, the environment becomes part of the intelligence.

Agents do not require ever larger exoskeletons of orchestration.

Instead, they inhabit well-designed worlds where reasoning becomes natural.

---

## Conclusion

The rapid evolution of AI infrastructure has revealed two distinct architectural paths.

The first path builds increasingly sophisticated harnesses around language models, coordinating agents through complex workflows and orchestration frameworks.

The second path focuses on designing environments where intelligence emerges naturally from structured workspaces.

Harnesses unlock capability through control.

Homes unlock capability through context.

Both approaches will likely play roles in the future of artificial intelligence.

But as systems grow more complex, the design of intelligent environments may prove to be one of the most powerful—and underexplored—directions for AI architecture.

Rather than asking how to better control artificial intelligence, we may instead ask a simpler question:

**What kind of world allows intelligence to flourish?**
